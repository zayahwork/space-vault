> Dr. Kelso,
>
> This is far more than I hoped for — thank you. I went through the SMOPS keynote and the IAC-25
> conjunction-assessment paper, and then spent real time in the first-gp-by-launch data.
>
> Rather than just eyeball it, I pulled the whole table and computed the median days from launch
> to first GP by year: 0.65 (2019), 0.88 (2022), 1.15 (2024), 1.58 (2025), and 2.71 so far in
> 2026. Median, not worst case — it has risen every year since 2021 and more than quadrupled since
> 2019. I'd assumed identification was largely a solved problem; watching the *typical* launch get
> steadily slower was not what I expected.
>
> The rideshares are the tail you'd predict from your 2017 paper. Transporter-17 (Jul 7) took
> 12.62 days to first GP — the worst of all 156 launches catalogued in 2026 — and it's still
> carried as OBJECT A with source TBD two weeks on. Transporter-16 from March is also still
> OBJECT A/TBD, which I take as a live instance of your "some never."
>
> What struck me most on the 2017 paper is that you solved the cooperative case: given independent
> operator orbits, the RMS-over-several-orbits assignment separates matches from non-matches by
> orders of magnitude and runs in about a minute. The part that's still open nine years later is
> the case where that second data set simply doesn't exist. That's a much harder problem than the
> one I thought you were describing, and I'd rather understand it properly than assume otherwise.
>
> The IAC-25 paper also corrected something I had wrong. I'd been treating catalog error as noise
> to be filtered. Short fit spans chosen specifically to avoid propagating through unmodeled
> maneuvers — and eGP meaning SP inherits the same blind spot — is a different problem entirely,
> and one that's structurally unkind to anyone trying to detect maneuvers from GP data alone. I'd
> been about to learn that the expensive way.
>
> And yes on awareness — I'll point people at the CelesTrak publications and the daily numbers.
> What you show quantitatively deserves to be seen against the "it's all under control" story.
>
