## What happened today (research window, evening)

4. **The detector was graded by outsiders for the first time.** I built a set of **24 real
   maneuvers that other people documented and dated** — Northrop press releases, a Space
   Force breakup confirmation, Space-Track's official reentry records, and McDowell's
   independent catalog — and scored us against them. Every row is marked *verified* (a
   named source states it) or *assumed* (we inferred it). **We catch 10 of 14 scoreable
   GEO maneuvers**, and the biggest catches are 600–700× above the noise floor, which I
   measured rather than picked.
   → [[RESULTS - Ground Truth]]

   Three things came out of it that we didn't know this morning:
   - **Yesterday's GEO explanation was wrong and I've retracted it.** We said the verifier
     misses GEO because it watches altitude and GEO burns don't change altitude. They do.
     And the proposed fix — switching to longitude drift — turns out to be the *same
     number* rewritten. So **why SES shows no signal is an open question again.**
   - **The actual bug is a timing window, not the physics.** We only count a burn if it
     shows up within 3 days. The catalog can be 10 days late. Changing one number from
     3 to 14 takes us from 71% to 86%.
   - **Galaxy 15 — the famous "zombiesat", the exact story in our pitch — is one we'd
     have missed.** Nothing dramatic happened to its orbit; it just *stopped* making its
     regular corrections. That can't be caught by looking for a big jump. It's the strongest
     evidence yet that `quiet.py` (Jul 29) is the right build, and that the current
     verifier will never carry the "went quiet" claim by itself.

   One correction to yesterday's picture: **the detector can only be scored on 12 hours of
   data, not two days.** The public-catalog half of the archive only starts at the 07-22
   0200Z snapshot.

## Jargon, once
