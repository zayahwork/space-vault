**4. Did a big research dive.** The short version:

- **Our idea, in one sentence:** teach a computer every satellite's normal routine
  (**pattern of life**), then automatically flag when one breaks its routine (a **maneuver** —
  when a satellite fires thrusters and moves). Think: security camera for orbit.
- **Why that instead of collision warnings:** the government now gives basic collision warnings
  away FREE (a system called **TraCSS**). Never compete with free. We sell the smarter layer:
  *"which warning actually matters, and is that other satellite acting weird?"*
- **Someone big already does this — and that's okay.** A company called **Slingshot** sells a
  fancy version (product named "Agatha") to governments for big money. Our angle: the same idea,
  **way cheaper, using only free public data**, for all the customers Slingshot ignores — small
  satellite operators, insurance companies, and government small-business contracts.
- **People really do pay:** a competitor (Kayhan) publicly charges **~$2,000–2,500/month** to
  small operators. So a one-person company needs only a handful of customers to be real.
- **The military literally asked for this:** the Space Force's stated 2026 goal is "predictive
  **SDA**" — predicting satellite *behavior*, not just cataloging positions. That's our product
  described in their own words. And there's a program (**SBIR**) that PAYS small companies to
  build things like this — that's our funding plan.
- **A lucky timing break:** on July 11 the space catalog passed 100,000 objects, and the old
  data format (**TLE**) literally can't count that high. Tons of old software is breaking right
  now. We're starting fresh on the new format (**OMM**), so we skip the whole mess — and it
  gives us a great conversation-starter.
- **A free practice dataset exists:** MIT published **SPLID** — satellite movement data where
  experts already labeled every maneuver. Like a practice exam WITH the answer key. Our
  prototype trains on that, so the Friday demo is realistic.
