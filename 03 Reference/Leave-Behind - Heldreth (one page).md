# 🛰️ Knowing when a satellite stops behaving like itself

**Zayah Nelson** · Snohomish, WA · 425-409-8684 · zayahwork@gmail.com

## Who I am

One ML engineer, working alone, in Snohomish. No sensors, no telescopes, no radar — I use
only the free public tracking data that the US government publishes for every object in
orbit. No funding, no product to sell you, and no vendor's story to protect. That last part
is the point: I have no reason to tell you the data says something it doesn't.

## What I do, in one sentence

I watch public tracking data and flag the satellites that have **stopped behaving like
themselves** — moved when they shouldn't have, or stopped moving when they always used to.

## The number I can stand behind

Anyone can flag satellites. The hard part is proving the flags mean something, so I checked
my own list against evidence it never saw:

> Take my top-ranked suspects. Then take a **control group** — ordinary satellites, matched
> for age and orbit, picked at random. Now go to the catalog's own altitude history and ask
> which ones actually show a burn signature.
>
> **About 68–72% of my suspects clear that bar. About 10% of the controls do.**

Same result on two independent snapshots taken six hours apart, and the *entire* flagged
list — all ~490 objects, nothing cherry-picked — still verifies at nearly **4× the control
rate**. Weaker at the bottom of the list than the top, which is what a working ranking is
supposed to look like.

**What I am not claiming:** this is the catalog's history checking my catalog analysis. It is
not the operators confirming their own burns. Two independent methods agreeing is strong; it
isn't proof, and I'd rather you hear that from me than find it later.

## The signal I think underwriters actually want: *went quiet*

Everything above detects **movement**. The more valuable signal is an **absence**.

A healthy GEO satellite holds its slot with small station-keeping burns on a regular rhythm —
it's the most predictable behavior in orbit. When a satellite **stops** doing that, something
is usually wrong: propellant gone, a thruster failed, attitude control lost, or the bus is
dying. It then drifts out of its assigned slot.

For a GEO fleet, the same signal reads three ways:

- **Early warning.** Trouble is visible in the behavior before it becomes a claim, a service
  outage, or a customer's angry phone call.
- **Independent verification.** Policy language is starting to reference maneuver behavior —
  avoidance-action counts, disposal obligations. Someone has to be able to *check* those
  clauses without taking the operator's word for it.
- **Portfolio view.** It works on satellites nobody gave me permission to watch, because the
  data is public. That means a fleet, a competitor's fleet, or an entire orbital shell.

**Honest status:** the went-quiet detector is built and tested, and it currently **refuses to
run** — judging a satellite's rhythm needs about a week of banked history and I have a few
days. It unblocks itself around **29 July 2026**. The verified numbers above come from low
Earth orbit, where the traffic is; GEO is the same method on a slower, cleaner rhythm, and I
have not demonstrated it yet. I'd rather show you that gap than paper over it.

## The ask — a fleet to watch, not money

**Name me a fleet.** Ideally GEO, roughly 10–40 satellites, insured by someone you place with.

I watch it for 60 days and send you a plain-English report once a month: what moved, what
stopped moving, what looks abnormal, and what I got wrong. **No invoice, no contract, no data
or access needed from you or the operator.** Everything I use is already public.

If the reports turn out to be useless, you tell me and we stop — you've lost one email a
month. If they're not useless, you saw it before the market did, and you'll have a year of
evidence about whether behavior data belongs in pricing while everyone else is still
arguing about it.
