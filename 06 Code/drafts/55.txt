Your SDC9 paper with Cipollone and Di Lizia names the problem I keep hitting: there aren't enough manoeuvre histories to label, so supervised methods don't generalise, and you went unsupervised with a Bi-LSTM autoencoder on [a, e, i, omega, B*] instead. The part I found most useful was the honesty about the limitation - TLE noise degrades the reconstruction, and the semi-supervised Sentinel-3A/3B campaign was the only place you had something like truth.

I've come at the same wedge from a cruder angle and hit the same wall. I rank LEO objects by disagreement between the public catalog and the operators' own published ephemerides; about 4,900 Starlink objects look like they moved, but 89% of that is the catalog entry being stale rather than the spacecraft moving. What's left is roughly 490 station-keepers that moved when they shouldn't have. No labels there either.

So my scoring workaround, and the thing I'd like your opinion on: instead of labels I built a matched control group - same shell, same altitude band, same epoch window, objects with no reason to have manoeuvred - and checked both groups against 30 days of altitude history. As of 22 July, ~68-72% of the top suspects show a real burn signature against ~10% of matched controls, and I've been treating that ~10% as an empirical false positive rate. Two of my own methods agreeing, not operator ground truth. Is a matched-control base rate a defensible way to score an unsupervised detector when no manoeuvre log exists, or does it just move the assumption somewhere less visible? You've thought harder about the no-labels problem than I have.
